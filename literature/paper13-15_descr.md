##### **Amino acid composition drives aggregation during peptide synthesis. Tamás, B., Alberts, M., Laino, T., Hartrampf, N. *et al.*, Nat. Chem. 18, 677–685 (2026). https://doi.org/10.1038/s41557-026-02090-0**

1. This paper investigates the relationship between amino acid composition and peptide aggregation during solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS). Using machine learning models trained on experimental and literature data, the goal is to predict whether a peptide sequence will aggregate during synthesis. A key finding is that overall amino acid composition plays a more important role than sequence order, suggesting that aggregation is largely governed by global physicochemical properties rather than local sequence motifs. The study provides both predictive models and chemical insight into aggregation behavior.
2. Size is 539 peptide sequences with its source being experimental SPPS and literature data. Binary lables were used (aggregation vs non-aggregating). For features, amino acid composition, physiochemical properties and descriptors were used.
3. Many different models were used together with different data encoding. Feature based models with the input being the amino acid sequence.
4. Compared models based on accuracy (All models were trained with fivefold cross-validation (that is, the data are randomly split into five folds and training is repeated five times using a different fold as the test set) and the performance of each model was assessed using the accuracy. After the average was taken). More complex models (e.g. language models) did not significantly outperform simpler approaches. Outcome: aggregation not strongly squence dependent but instead depend mainly onamino acid composition, hydrophobic content and physiochemical properties.

##### **Continuous flow synthesis of pyridinium salts accelerated by multi-objective Bayesian optimization with active learning, Baldwin, L., A. *et al.*, Chem. Sci., 2023, 14, 8061-8069 (https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlelanding/2023/sc/d3sc01303k)**

1. This paper presents a machine learning assisted optimization of a chemical reaction using a human-in-the-loop workflow. The authors optimize the continuous flow synthesis of butylpyridinium bromide by applying multi-objective Bayesian optimization (EDBO+), aiming to simultaneously maximize reaction yield and production rate. The method efficiently explores a large reaction parameter space and identifies optimal trade-offs (Pareto front) with very few experiments. Reactions were performed in a continuous flow reactor, where the algorithm suggested new experiments based on previous results.
2. 75 experiments (around 30 optimization rounds) with inpit features being temnperature, residence time and mole fraction. Output is reaction yield. Data was measured by NMR. Small data set typically used in Bayesian Optimization.
3. Main model used: EDBO+ , a multi-objective Bayesian optimization platform. Additonally, not fully automated process, human in the loop still was an important aspect.
4. The paper does not compare EDBO+ directly against any other ML models. The main advantage shown is that EDBO+ could efficiently find good trade off conditions with only a small number of experiments, Outcome: EDBO+ effective for continuous-flow reaction optimization which generates a Pareto front balancing yield and productivity.

##### **Enhancing Retrosynthesis Prediction with Distillation Learning, Yiping Liu, Zhou Yu, Jiayi Zhang, Yujie Chen, Shu Wu, Leyi Wei, Xiangxiang Zeng, Yuyan Han, and Yuansheng Liu., J. Chem. Inf. Model., 2026 66 (7), 3774-3786 (https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acs.jcim.6c00322)**

1. This paper presents a deep learning approach to single-step retrosynthesis prediction, where the goal is to predict reactant molecules from a given product. The authors improve transformer-based retrosynthesis models by introducing distillation learning strategies, including mutual distillation and self-distillation. These approaches enhance model performance, especially for low-resource reaction classes. The study demonstrates that knowledge sharing between models leads to more accurate and robust retrosynthesis predictions, addressing key challenges such as data imbalance in chemical reaction datasets.
2. USPTO-50K (~50,000 reactions), USPTO-Full (~1 million reactions). Data are chemical reactions represented as SMILES. Input are molecules and the outputs are the reactants. Features are tokenized SMILES sequences (Transfer learning)
3. - Model type: Transformer-based neural networks for template-free single-step retrosynthesis prediction.¨
   - Architecture: Encoder–decoder Transformer sequence-to-sequence model.
   - Input: Product molecule as SMILES strings.
   - Output: Predicted reactant SMILES strings.
   - Specific proposed models: Retro-MD1(One of the two Transformer models in the mutual distillation framework.), Retro-MD2 (The second Transformer model in the mutual distillation framework.), and Retro-SD (A self-distillation Transformer model).
   - Training strategy: Retro-MD trains two Transformer models at the same time and lets them learn from each other. Retro-SD trains one Transformer model that learns from its own earlier best versions.
4. Metrics used: Top-k accuracy (Top-1, Top-3, Top-5, Top-10) to compare models. The main outcome is that  distillation learning improves retrosynthesis prediction , especially for low-resource reaction classes. Overall, the paper demonstrates that  knowledge distillation can reduce performance gaps between high-resource and low-resource reaction classes , making retrosynthesis prediction more robust and useful for practical synthesis planning.
