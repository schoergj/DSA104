# From Reaction Stoichiometry to Life Cycle Assessment: Decision Tree-Based Estimation Tool

**Authors:** Tim Langhorst, Benedikt Winter, Moritz Tuchschmid, Dennis Roskosch, André Bardow
**Journal:** ACS Environmental Au, 2025, 5, 550–560
**DOI:** 10.1021/acsenvironau.4c00065

## 1. Summary

This paper proposes a set of regression decision trees that estimate the most important inputs for an industrial-scale Life Cycle Inventory (LCI) of a chemical process directly from the reaction stoichiometry and simple thermo-physical reactant/product properties. The authors deliberately move away from predicting aggregate cradle-to-gate impacts (as done by most ML-LCA approaches) and instead estimate the underlying technical flows, which are easier to interpret, modular, transparent, and independent of LCA methodological choices (system boundaries, allocation, impact category). The decision trees are designed to replace the generic proxy values currently used in LCI databases when only the reaction equation is known, and the authors explicitly aim to reach an LCA accuracy comparable to the AACE cost-estimation accuracy class for early-stage R&D. The tool is published as an open-source Python toolbox plus short manually-usable trees on GitHub.

## 2. Dataset

The training dataset is derived from the IHS Markit Process Economics Programme (PEP) Yearbook, restricted to organic chemical reactions with chemically well-defined inputs (excluding biochemical and electrochemical routes due to insufficient sample sizes). The final dataset contains **409 processes covering 148 unique main products**, ranging from platform chemicals (butadiene, ethylene, methanol) through intermediates (isopropanol) to consumer chemicals (BHT, pyromellitic dianhydride). Molecular weights span 26 g/mol (acetylene) to 270 g/mol (alachlor). The dataset's overall GWI distribution has a median of 3.8 kg CO₂-eq/kg_product and a mean of 5.5 kg CO₂-eq/kg_product. The authors selected 16 input features after a leave-one-out feature importance pre-study: 10 molecular features (four boiling points — min/max of reactants and products, molecular weight of main product, and atom counts for Cl, F, N, non-cyclic C, and cyclic C) and 6 reaction-based features (number of reactants and products, mol H₂ consumed, presence of additional side products, stoichiometric water formation, molar fraction of main product). Boiling points were obtained from COSMO-RS calculations.

## 3. Models

Seven separate regression decision trees were trained, one for each LCI target: (1) raw materials coefficient C_rm (a yield-like factor scaling stoichiometric raw material demands), (2) direct GHG emissions in CO₂-eq, and demands for (3) electricity, (4) steam, (5) natural gas, (6) cooling water, and (7) process water. The implementation uses DecisionTreeRegressor from scikit-learn 0.23.2 (Python 3.8) with a modified CART algorithm. Mean Absolute Error (MAE) was used as the splitting criterion (rather than the default MSE), making predictions the *median* of each node and more robust to outliers. To avoid overfitting, two hyperparameters were constrained based on a pre-study: maximum tree depth = 7 (allowing up to 128 terminal nodes) and minimum 4 samples per node (~1% of the training set). Impact characterization for the GWI used ReCiPe (H) V1.13 with ecoinvent 3.5 background data (cut-off, global market mixes), and a deliberately high-impact lignite electricity scenario was used to avoid overstating accuracy.

## 4. Evaluation and Outcome

Performance was assessed via leave-one-out cross-validation, with two metrics borrowed from cost engineering: the MAE and the share of processes outside the AACE target accuracy range of −50% to +100%. Compared to the best available default proxies (Geisler et al. for yield, Kim & Overcash for energy), the decision trees reduce the MAE of the overall GWI estimate by >20% and the share of processes outside the AACE range by >40% (only 11% fall outside). On individual technical flows, the trees improve MAE by 8–24% and reduce out-of-range processes by 17–31%. For the raw-materials GWI alone, the median ratio of predicted/benchmark moved from 1.022 (proxy) to 1.003 (tree), with the interquartile range tightening from −15%/+11% to −7%/+6%. The heating-demand prediction shifted its mode from 0.59 to 0.89, eliminating the systematic underestimation produced by proxies, and the electricity prediction collapsed a bimodal proxy distribution into a single mode at 0.86. The authors also showed that the trees' splits align with chemical intuition (e.g., distinguishing oxidative dehydrogenation from dehydration via boiling-point and water-formation criteria, and C1-chemistry routes via reactant boiling points), supporting their interpretability. Remaining limitations: no prediction of direct non-GHG emissions, no explicit catalyst/auxiliary modelling, and continued dependence on PEP-data quality.
