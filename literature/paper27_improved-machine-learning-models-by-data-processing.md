# Improved Machine Learning Models by Data Processing for Predicting Life-Cycle Environmental Impacts of Chemicals

**Authors:** Ye Sun, Xiuheng Wang, Nanqi Ren, Yanbiao Liu, Shijie You
**Journal:** Environmental Science & Technology, 2023, 57, 3434–3444
**DOI:** 10.1021/acs.est.2c04945

## 1. Summary

The paper tackles two persistent weaknesses of ML models used to predict life-cycle environmental impacts of chemicals from molecular descriptors: low prediction accuracy and poor interpretability. Rather than focusing on the ML architecture itself, the authors concentrate on data processing. They propose (i) a hybrid filter–wrapper feature-selection method combining Mutual Information and Permutation Importance (MI-PI) to identify a small, informative subset of molecular descriptors while preserving their original physicochemical meaning (in contrast to PCA, which generates abstract new variables), and (ii) a weighted Euclidean distance scheme that selects, for each prediction target, the subset of training chemicals most structurally similar to that target. These two techniques are combined with an ANN regressor (denoted MPEN), and the resulting models are explained via SHAP. The framework is demonstrated across six ReCiPe Midpoint (H) impact categories.

## 2. Dataset

The dataset consists of 187 organic chemicals extracted from the ecoinvent v3.8 LCI database, covering aromatics, dyes, pharmaceuticals, and halogenated hydrocarbons. For each chemical, six impact categories serve as separate prediction targets: Global Warming (GWP), Human Toxicity (HTP), Metal Depletion (MDP), Freshwater Ecotoxicity (FETP), Particulate Matter Formation (PMFP), and Terrestrial Acidification (TAP). Molecular descriptors are generated from each chemical's SMILES code by the PaDEL-Descriptor software, producing 1,444 raw descriptors spanning atom counts, electrotopological state indices, ring counts, molecular linear free-energy relations, and more. After preprocessing — removing zero-variance descriptors and one of every pair with Pearson correlation > 0.95, followed by z-score normalization — 531 descriptors remain (the "FULL" dataset). The MI-PI feature selection further compresses these to 58–86 descriptors depending on the impact category (e.g., 67 for GWP, 58 for HTP). For each target chemical, the weighted Euclidean distance then selects the 60% most similar training chemicals as a target-specific training subset. The data is split 90% training / 10% test.

## 3. Models

The authors first benchmarked four ML algorithms — Support Vector Machine (SVM), Random Forest (RF), XGBoost, and Artificial Neural Network (ANN) — on the FULL preprocessed dataset, with the ANN emerging as the best performer across the six impact categories (SVM performed poorly due to the wide range of feature values undermining its distance-based margin). The final model architecture is a feed-forward ANN implemented in Python 3.7, with hyperparameters tuned via GridSearch with 10-fold cross-validation in scikit-learn. The training pipeline involves three stages: (1) MI screens out redundant features, (2) PI further prunes features based on their measured contribution to model accuracy, and (3) the weighted Euclidean distance (with weights set to normalized PI scores) selects the most relevant 60% of training chemicals for each prediction target. The fully-improved model is denoted MPEN (MI-PI + Euclidean + Neural Network). Model interpretation uses the SHAP (Shapley Additive Explanation) method to quantify each descriptor's contribution to each impact category prediction.

## 4. Evaluation and Outcome

Performance was measured by R² and RMSE on the held-out test set. The MPEN models substantially outperformed both the FULL/PCA baselines and prior published ANN-based LCA predictors. On the test set, the MPEN models achieved R² = 0.81 (GWP), 0.81 (HTP), 0.84 (MDP), 0.75 (FETP), 0.73 (PMFP), and 0.86 (TAP), with corresponding RMSE values (e.g., 0.99 for GWP). For comparison, the previous benchmark (Song et al., 2017, using PCA-reduced descriptors) reached only R² = 0.48 for GWP and 0.71 for HTP. Feature-selection ablations showed that MI-PI clearly outperforms PCA, MI alone, and PI alone, and that MI-PI's runtime (~47 s) is two orders of magnitude faster than PI alone (~366 s) while retaining accuracy. Replacing the full training set with weighted-Euclidean-selected subsets raised the GWP R² from 0.75 (MPN) to 0.81 (MPEN), and cosine-similarity and unweighted-Euclidean alternatives were both inferior. Validation on four held-out chemicals (phenol, tetrahydrofuran, cyanoacetic acid, adipic acid) gave relative errors of 1.3%–3.1% on GWP, vs. 5.1%–10.7% for the MPN baseline. SHAP analysis confirmed physicochemical plausibility — e.g., AATS1i (linked to first ionization energy and thus atmospheric lifetime) correlates positively with GWP, and chlorine-atom-related descriptors dominate HTP predictions. The authors acknowledge that the small dataset (187 samples) limits generalizability and that the molecular descriptors, while preserved, can still be hard to interpret intuitively.
