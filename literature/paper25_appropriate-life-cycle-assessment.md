# APPROPRIATE Life Cycle Assessment: A PROcess-Specific, PRedictive Impact AssessmenT Method for Emerging Chemical Processes

**Authors:** Johanna Kleinekorte, Jonas Kleppich, Lorenz Fleitmann, Verena Beckert, Luise Blodau, André Bardow
**Journal:** ACS Sustainable Chemistry & Engineering, 2023, 11, 9303–9319
**DOI:** 10.1021/acssuschemeng.2c07682

## 1. Summary

The paper introduces APPROPRIATE, a fully automated, predictive Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) framework designed to evaluate cradle-to-gate environmental impacts of emerging chemical processes at Technology Readiness Level (TRL) 2, where only the molecular structure of the main product and a gross reaction equation are known. The framework addresses two key shortcomings of prior predictive LCA approaches: low prediction accuracy due to limited LCA training data, and the inability to distinguish between alternative process routes producing the same chemical. To this end, the authors combine an encoder–decoder neural network (yielding a condensed latent molecular representation via transfer learning) with newly developed *process descriptors* (e.g. the stoichiometric sum of the reactants' impacts, automatized flowsheeting-derived energy demands), and feed these features into a Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) model. The framework not only predicts the global warming impact (GWI) but also quantifies prediction uncertainty, making efforts to be suitable as a decision-support tool in early process development.

## 2. Dataset

The LCA target dataset was constructed from the IHS Markit Process Economics Yearbook, starting from 1,692 German chemical processes. After filtering out processes without a clearly defined reaction equation, averaging duplicates, removing outliers (k-means cluster analysis), and excluding processes with GWI > 20 kg CO₂-eq, the final consistent dataset contains 304 unique processes producing 166 unique chemicals (50 aromatic, 116 aliphatic). The target variable is the GWI per kg of product. The encoder–decoder networks were trained separately on the much larger QM9 and ZINC cheminformatics databases (several thousand molecules) via transfer learning to overcome LCA data scarcity. Features fall into three groups: (i) condensed molecular descriptors (a learned latent vector of size d = 20), (ii) classical molecular descriptors (UNIFAC groups, atom/bond counts, thermodynamic properties from Gaussian/COSMO-RS), and (iii) process descriptors (stoichiometric impact sum, ΔRG, ΔRH, number of byproducts, automatized flowsheeting energy demands). The data was split 70/10/20 (train/validation/test), with splits chosen via minimum Kullback–Leibler divergence to ensure distributional similarity across subsets.

## 3. Models

Two encoder–decoder architectures were compared for generating the latent molecular representation: a CNN2RNN autoencoder (convolutional encoder + recurrent decoder, reconstructing SMILES) and a GNN2RNN translator (graph neural network encoder translating a molecular graph into SMILES via a recurrent decoder). The GNN2RNN outperformed the CNN2RNN (average cross-entropy 0.63 vs. 1.04) and was selected for the framework. For the regression step, two models were compared: a Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) implemented in scikit-learn, with kernels optimized via Duvenaud's Automatic Model Construction (resulting in SE·Lin² + Lin² for the process-specific dataset), and an Artificial Neural Network (ANN) implemented in TensorFlow with hyperparameters tuned via Optuna (sigmoid activation, one hidden layer of 308 neurons for the process-specific case). Feature selection used sequential forward selection; inputs were standardized using a Robust Scaler.

## 4. Evaluation and Outcome

On the random test set, the GPR-based framework using process descriptors plus the latent representation achieved the highest performance with R² = 0.61 and RMSE = 1.76 kg CO₂-eq, substantially outperforming the component-specific ANN baseline representing prior state-of-the-art (R² = 0.30). Averaged over 30 randomized splits (following Wernet et al.'s evaluation protocol), the process-specific latent model reached R² = 0.53. Feature importance analysis (Breiman's permutation method) identified the stoichiometric sum of reactants' impacts (18%) and specific latent dimensions (notably dimension 7, which correlates with aromaticity) as the most informative features. The framework correctly identified the lower-impact alternative in 18 of 21 (86%) of process comparisons and successfully captured background system changes in a CO₂-based methanol case study, where a component-specific ANN failed. The authors conclude that feature engineering matters more than the regression model choice and that improved process modeling (better energy estimates, side-reaction handling) is the most promising direction for future improvement.
